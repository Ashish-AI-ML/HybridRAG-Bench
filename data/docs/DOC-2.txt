DOC-2: David Deutsch and the Quantum Turing Machine (1985)
==========================================================

In 1985, the British physicist David Deutsch published a paper in the Proceedings of the Royal Society of London titled "Quantum Theory, the Church-Turing Principle and the Universal Quantum Computer." This paper is widely regarded as the formal birth of quantum computing as a mathematical discipline, providing the rigorous theoretical framework that Feynman's 1981 proposal had left unspecified.

Deutsch's central contribution was the formalization of the quantum Turing machine (QTM). The classical Turing machine, conceived by Alan Turing in 1936, had served as the foundational model for all of classical computation: it established that any computable function could be executed by a sufficiently simple abstract machine reading and writing symbols on a tape. Deutsch extended this model into the quantum domain by replacing the classical states of the Turing machine with quantum superpositions. A quantum Turing machine could, in principle, process multiple computational paths simultaneously by exploiting quantum superposition and interference, rather than evaluating one path at a time as classical machines do.

Beyond the QTM model itself, Deutsch made a second significant contribution: he described the first quantum algorithm that demonstrated a concrete, provable advantage over any classical algorithm for a specific problem. Known as the Deutsch algorithm (later generalized by Richard Jozsa into the Deutsch-Jozsa algorithm), it determined a global property of a boolean function — whether the function was constant or balanced — in a single quantum query, whereas a classical computer required two queries in the worst case. Although the practical utility of this specific problem was limited, its theoretical importance was enormous: it was the first proof that quantum computation could solve a well-defined problem strictly faster than any classical computation, establishing quantum speedup as a mathematically real phenomenon rather than mere conjecture.

Deutsch also introduced the concept of a universal quantum computer — a quantum machine capable of simulating any other quantum machine, analogous to the role of the universal Turing machine in classical computation. This concept provided the theoretical justification for building general-purpose quantum hardware rather than specialized simulators.

The 1985 paper established the mathematical vocabulary — quantum gates, quantum circuits, computational basis states, and quantum parallelism — that subsequent researchers including Shor and Grover would use to construct their own algorithms. Deutsch's work transformed quantum computing from a physicist's informal suggestion into a rigorous branch of theoretical computer science.
