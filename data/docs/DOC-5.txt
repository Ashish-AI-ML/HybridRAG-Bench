DOC-5: Early Quantum Hardware — IBM and D-Wave Systems
=======================================================

The translation of quantum computing theory into physical hardware accelerated significantly in the late 1990s and early 2000s, with two organizations pursuing fundamentally different approaches: IBM Research and the Canadian startup D-Wave Systems.

IBM's early quantum hardware efforts focused on nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) based quantum computing, which encodes qubits in the nuclear spin states of atoms within carefully chosen molecules dissolved in liquid solution. In 2001, a team at IBM's Almaden Research Center, led by Isaac Chuang, successfully executed Shor's algorithm on a 7-qubit NMR quantum computer using a molecule of perfluorobutadienyl iron complex dissolved in a deuterated acetone solvent. The machine correctly factored the number 15 into its prime factors 3 and 5. Although factoring 15 is trivially simple for any classical device, the demonstration was a landmark proof that Shor's algorithm could be physically executed on real quantum hardware. NMR-based systems were subsequently found to be unscalable: controlling more than roughly 10 qubits through NMR techniques proved impractical due to the difficulty of isolating individual spin states as system size grows, and the approach was largely abandoned by the research community.

D-Wave Systems, founded in 1999 in Burnaby, British Columbia, pursued a radically different paradigm. Rather than building a universal gate-model quantum computer capable of executing arbitrary quantum algorithms, D-Wave developed quantum annealing hardware. Quantum annealing is a metaheuristic optimization process that exploits quantum tunnelling to search for the global minimum of a cost function, making it well-suited for certain combinatorial optimization problems but fundamentally incapable of running general algorithms such as Shor's or Grover's.

In 2011, D-Wave released the D-Wave One, marketed as the world's first commercially available quantum computer, with 128 superconducting flux qubits. In 2013, D-Wave Two with 512 qubits followed. These systems attracted significant commercial investment, including purchases by Lockheed Martin and a joint acquisition by Google and NASA. However, independent academic studies through 2014 and 2015 found that D-Wave's hardware showed no consistent quantum speedup over classical optimization algorithms on the tested problem sets, and the debate over whether D-Wave systems demonstrated genuine quantum advantage continued throughout the decade. The fundamental architectural divergence between D-Wave's annealing approach and the universal gate model pursued by IBM, Google, and academic groups remained a defining tension in early quantum hardware discourse.
