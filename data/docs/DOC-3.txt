DOC-3: Shor's Algorithm and Its Cryptographic Impact (1994)
============================================================

In 1994, the mathematician Peter Shor, working at AT&T Bell Laboratories, published an algorithm that sent immediate shockwaves through both the theoretical computer science and cybersecurity communities. Shor's algorithm demonstrated that a sufficiently powerful quantum computer could factor large composite integers in polynomial time — a feat considered computationally intractable for classical computers, which require exponential time under the best-known classical algorithms.

The significance of this result was not purely mathematical. The security of RSA encryption — the dominant public-key cryptographic protocol used to secure internet communications, financial transactions, and government data — rests entirely on the classical hardness of integer factorization. RSA encryption works because multiplying two large prime numbers is trivial, while recovering those primes from their product is computationally infeasible for classical machines given sufficient key length. Shor's algorithm directly invalidated this assumption for quantum adversaries: a quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could, in principle, break RSA encryption by factoring the public key in feasible time, rendering vast swaths of digital security infrastructure vulnerable.

The algorithm works by reducing the factoring problem to the problem of finding the period of a modular arithmetic function, a task at which quantum computers excel through quantum Fourier transform operations. The quantum Fourier transform exploits superposition and interference to extract global periodic properties of a function exponentially faster than any classical discrete Fourier transform can. This is the source of the exponential speedup: not brute-force parallelism, but a structured quantum interference pattern that amplifies the correct period while cancelling incorrect guesses.

The publication of Shor's algorithm in 1994 catalyzed a dramatic increase in government and institutional funding for quantum computing research worldwide. Intelligence agencies, defence ministries, and technology companies recognized that the long-term viability of existing cryptographic infrastructure depended on understanding and potentially countering quantum computing capabilities. It also stimulated the field of post-quantum cryptography — the development of cryptographic protocols resistant to quantum attacks — which remains an active area of standardization efforts at institutions including NIST.

Shor's algorithm is classified as providing an exponential speedup over the best known classical algorithms for factoring, distinguishing it fundamentally from algorithms like Grover's search, which provides only a quadratic speedup.
