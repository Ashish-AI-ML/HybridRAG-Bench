DOC-1: Feynman's 1981 Proposal for Quantum Simulation
=======================================================

In 1981, the physicist Richard Feynman delivered a landmark keynote address at the First Conference on the Physics of Computation, held jointly at MIT and Tufts University. In this talk, later formalized in a 1982 paper titled "Simulating Physics with Computers," Feynman articulated a problem that had long frustrated computational physicists: classical boolean computers are fundamentally incapable of efficiently simulating quantum mechanical systems.

The core of Feynman's argument rested on the exponential resource problem. When a classical computer attempts to simulate a quantum system containing N particles, the number of variables required to describe the full quantum state grows exponentially as 2^N. For even a modest system of 40 particles, the memory and processing requirements exceed the practical capacity of any conceivable classical machine. Feynman observed that this was not merely an engineering limitation but a deep structural mismatch between the discrete, deterministic nature of classical boolean logic and the probabilistic, superposition-based nature of quantum mechanics.

Feynman's proposed solution was conceptually elegant: build a computer that is itself quantum mechanical. Such a machine would represent information using quantum bits, or qubits, which can exist in superpositions of 0 and 1 simultaneously. Because the simulating machine would obey the same quantum rules as the system it models, the simulation could proceed with resources that scale polynomially rather than exponentially. In essence, Feynman argued that nature, being quantum, should be simulated by a quantum device.

This proposal was deliberately theoretical. Feynman did not specify a physical implementation, a set of quantum gates, or an error correction strategy. He was laying out a conceptual challenge: that a new class of computing machine, governed by quantum rather than classical physics, was both necessary and potentially achievable. His argument was not framed as a refutation of classical computing in general, but as an identification of a specific domain — quantum physics simulation — where classical computing was provably inadequate.

The 1982 paper is widely recognized as the intellectual origin point of quantum computing as a research field. It established the motivation that would drive theorists like David Deutsch, Peter Shor, and Lov Grover to formalize the mathematical foundations of quantum computation in the years that followed. Feynman's framing of the exponential simulation problem remains a standard pedagogical entry point for the field to this day, and his insight that quantum systems are most naturally modeled by quantum machines continues to underpin the research programs of major quantum computing laboratories worldwide.
